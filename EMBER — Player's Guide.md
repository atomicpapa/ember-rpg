# EMBER — Player's Guide

**Version:** 1.0 — see `CHANGELOG.md`

*Read this to understand how EMBER plays, without wading through the rules meant for the AI Game Master. This is for **you**, the player — you don't paste it to the AI (paste the Core Engine and a Genre Pack for that). It gives you the concept and the essentials; the Core Engine has the exact numbers if you ever want them.*

---

## The gist

EMBER is a solo roleplaying game where an **AI is your Game Master** and you play the hero. The AI paints the world, plays everyone in it, and decides what happens around you; **you** decide what your character does. There's no board and no video game — it's a story you build together, one choice at a time.

Here's the whole loop in a nutshell:

> **The AI describes a situation and asks what you do. You say it. When the outcome is genuinely in doubt, you roll two dice and add a number — and how high you roll decides whether things go well, go well *but* with a catch, or go wrong.** Then the AI narrates what happens and the story rolls on.

That's the entire engine. Everything below is just detail on those pieces.

**What you need:** two six-sided dice (**2d6**) — real or an app. You roll all the dice; the AI never does.

## How a session feels

Play is a conversation with a rhythm: the AI sets a scene and turns to you; you describe what your character tries; if it's uncertain and failure would be interesting, you roll; the AI tells you how it shakes out and what's happening now. Repeat. You never pick from menus — you just describe what you want to do, in plain language, and the AI works out the rest.

You can play a quick one-hour adventure or a saga across many sessions. On desktop with a filesystem connection, the AI writes your progress to disk automatically as you go (see `PLAYING-ON-DESKTOP.md`); on mobile or other setups without file access, you'll copy the save block back yourself at checkpoints (see `PLAYING-ON-MOBILE.md`). Either way, you can stop anytime and pick up right where you left off.

## Ready-made adventures

Don't want to build a scenario from scratch? Browse the `Scenarios/` folder for prebuilt **Scenario Packs** — ready-to-run adventures organized by genre, each with everything you need to jump straight into a story.

## Who your character is

You're defined by a few simple things:

- **Five stats** — **Might** (force), **Grace** (agility), **Wits** (smarts and perception), **Presence** (charm and command), and **Resolve** (willpower — and your sanity, in horror). Each is a small number that gets added to your dice.
- **An archetype** — your "class" for the genre (a Warrior, a Hedge-Mage, a Pilot, a Survivor...). It gives you a couple of special abilities only your kind of character has.
- **Tags** — short truths about you and your gear (*Ex-soldier, Silver-tongued, Enchanted blade*). When one fits what you're doing, it makes you better at it.
- **A Drive and a Bond** — a goal you're chasing and a connection you care about. Both give your character a reason to act, and both help you grow.

Making one takes about five minutes — or you can grab a ready-made character from your Genre Pack and start playing immediately.

## Rolling the dice

When the AI asks for a roll, roll **2d6 and add the stat that fits**. Read the total three ways:

- **High (10+) — it works cleanly.**
- **Middle (7–9) — it works, but at a cost.** A complication, a price, a hard choice, or only partway.
- **Low (6 or under) — it goes wrong,** and the AI throws a twist at you. (Consolation: you earn a point of experience — failing is how you grow.)

That **middle band is the heart of EMBER.** Most things you try will succeed — the drama is in what they *cost* you. You're never chasing a hidden target number; the AI simply gives you a small bonus when the odds favor you and a small penalty when they don't.

## The things you do

You don't memorize a rulebook of actions — you just describe what your character does, and the AI knows which rule applies. Under the hood, the common ones are: **facing danger**, **striking** a foe, **enduring** harm or fear, **reading** a person or situation to learn about it, **swaying** someone, and **recovering**. Your archetype adds a few signature moves that are yours alone.

## Getting hurt

Danger leaves a mark. You can take **Harm** (fill your track and you're out of the fight — though not always dead), pick up **Conditions** like *Shaken* or *Wounded* that nag at you until they clear, and occasionally earn a lasting **Scar** from something truly dramatic. Most of it heals between adventures; the memorable wounds — and your legend — stay with you.

## Growing over time

Your character gets better through play, not grinding. You earn experience by **missing rolls, chasing your Drive, and leaning into your Bond,** and you spend it to pick up new skills, learn new moves, or sharpen a stat. Growth reflects what you actually did in the story.

## Your character is yours to keep

Your character is saved to a file and **carries forward between adventures** — their abilities, gear, and reputation come along. Wounds heal, but their triumphs and scars travel with them. One catch: a character belongs to **one genre**. They can explore every flavor of it (a fantasy hero can go from high magic to gritty sword-and-sorcery), but a knight can't wander into a spaceship.

## A few tips

- **Describe, don't optimize.** Say what your character does and *why* — the color gives the AI more to play with.
- **Embrace the "cost" results.** The 7–9 moments are where the best stories happen.
- **Speak up about your tags.** If you think something about you or your gear should help, say so.
- **Missing isn't failing the game.** It hands you experience and hands the story a twist.

## Where this comes from

If EMBER feels familiar, that's on purpose. Its core is built on the **Powered by the Apocalypse** family of tabletop games — the 2d6 "success / success-at-a-cost / miss" spread and its fiction-first style come from **Apocalypse World** (D. Vincent Baker & Meguey Baker), with a nod to **Dungeon World** for its fantasy flavor and to **Ironsworn** (Shawn Tomkin) for solo play. EMBER adds its own genre-agnostic stats, scars, genre "lenses," persistent characters, and the design for being run by an AI Game Master. If you've enjoyed EMBER, those games are well worth your time.
